A note, before you go

Dear Anushka and Robin,

Japan in May is a gift: the cherry blossoms have fallen but the air is warm, the wisteria is in bloom, and the crowds of Golden Week have just dispersed. This is the country at its most composed. I've shaped the days below around a single idea — that you should never feel rushed, and never feel you've missed anything. The loudest sights are here, but so are the alley yakitori stalls, the deer who bow for crackers, and the long, unscheduled afternoons I've quietly left for you to find your own Japan.

Two dinners are extraordinary and require planning; I've flagged them. The rest is yours to bend — an hour shorter here, a morning swapped there. Consider this a frame, not a cage.

Bring comfortable shoes, a small cash wallet, and an appetite for fruit sandwiches at convenience stores at midnight. That, above all, is Japan.

With care, Your Concierge · April 2026

一
Day One · Tuesday
May 12
A soft landing.
Pace Gentle. Jetlag welcome. Nothing after dark.

Arrival in Kioicho.

You'll land at Narita in the afternoon. The hotel is a quiet tower above Akasaka, and your first hour in Japan should be spent doing almost nothing — unpacking, a window view, a walk to the nearest shrine. Save the famous sights for tomorrow; Japan is more generous when you meet it rested.

  • On arrival

    Narita to Kioicho — by Airport Limousine Bus or private car

    The easiest option after a long flight is a pre-booked private sedan — roughly 90 minutes door to door, ~¥28,000, arranged through the hotel concierge in advance. Otherwise, the Airport Limousine Bus from Narita runs directly to The Prince Gallery Tokyo Kioicho — 90 minutes, ¥3,600 per person, with luggage handled for you. Skip the Narita Express if you'd rather not change at Tokyo Station with bags.

    Before you leave the airport: buy a bottle of water, a melon pan from Lawson, and activate your Suica card on your iPhone Wallet. (See Moving Through Japan, below.)
  • 17:00

    Check in & settle

    Ask for a room facing the Imperial Palace grounds. Unpack slowly. A room-service pot of hōjicha if the kettle isn't calling to you.

  • 18:00

    A short pilgrimage — Hie Shrine 5 min on foot

    Just below the hotel, down a long arcade of vermilion torii gates banking up the hillside. It is still, usually empty at this hour, and exactly the right way to arrive in Japan — by passing beneath a hundred gates you didn't have to queue for. Ring the bell, clap twice, bow, and you're in.

  • 19:30

    First dinner — tonkatsu at a counter

    Butagumi Shokudo in Roppongi (15 minutes by taxi) serves the platonic ideal of a crispy pork cutlet on a plain wooden tray: rice, miso, shredded cabbage, pickles. Order the rosu (fatty) and hire (lean) to compare. ¥2,000 per person and no reservations. A first meal that is all comfort, no ceremony.

    Alternate: The basement floor of Tokyo Midtown is eight minutes away and has a dozen good ramen, soba, and donburi counters if you can't be bothered to move further.
  • 21:00

    Home, and early to bed

    Tomorrow starts at Meiji Shrine. Sleep is the point.

Concierge Note

If Anushka is curious, the hotel serves a proper afternoon tea on floor 30 until 17:00 — Japanese sweets and a view clear to Mount Fuji on a clean day.

二
Day Two · Wednesday
May 13
Classic Tokyo, unrushed.
Pace Full day on foot. Good shoes. An afternoon pause.

Shrines, backstreets & the world's busiest crossing.

A loop through the Tokyo most first-timers want to meet — but walked from the forest in, not the crowd in. We'll start at a shrine that feels like stepping into a different country altogether, drift through Harajuku's quieter lanes, and time the famous Shibuya Crossing to dusk, when it is prettiest and least ridiculous.

  • 07:30

    Breakfast — tamagoyaki and a melon

    Take the Marunouchi Line two stops to Tsukiji Outer Market (not Toyosu — that's the wholesale fish auction). It opens at dawn. Buy a grilled tamagoyaki skewer at Marutake, a fresh fruit sandwich at Sembikiya, and a half-melon for dessert at the fruit stand. Eat standing up.

    By 09:30 the tour groups arrive — we'll already be on the train.
  • 10:00

    Meiji Jingu — the forest in the middle of the city Shibuya

    A 700-meter walk through a canopied forest delivers you to the most important Shinto shrine in Tokyo. It's not old — the shrine was rebuilt in 1958 — but the forest around it was designed to feel ancient and does. Walk the wooded path without rushing.

  • 11:30

    Takeshita-dori & Cat Street for Anushka

    Come out the south gate and into Harajuku. Ten minutes on Takeshita Street is enough — the tourist rainbow crêpe, the photographs — then duck onto Cat Street behind it for the quiet vintage shops, pottery boutiques, and the Flying Tiger craft-supply wonderland that Anushka will not want to leave.

  • 13:00

    Lunch — Afuri yuzu-shio ramen

    Afuri Harajuku. A bright, citrus-scented chicken broth with a soft yolk egg. The antidote to heavy ramen. Pay at the vending machine, hand the ticket to the cook, slurp loudly. ¥1,500 per person.

  • 14:30

    An unhurried Omotesando stroll

    Japan's prettiest tree-lined avenue, and an open-air architecture museum — Tadao Ando's Omotesando Hills, the Prada crystal box, the Dior façade. Stop for matcha soft serve at Nanaya — seven grades of matcha intensity; level 7 is "plant, not drink." Both of you should order it.

    A free hour here. Wander. Sit on a bench. Do nothing.
  • 17:30

    Shibuya Crossing — from above, first

    The best view is from the 14th floor of Shibuya Sky (book a sunset slot online) or, free, from the Starbucks window on the second floor of Tsutaya. Then cross it yourself. Then cross it again. It's sillier and better than you'd expect.

  • 19:00

    Dinner — Omoide Yokocho

    Two subway stops north to Shinjuku. "Memory Lane" is a narrow alley of yakitori stalls from the 1950s, smoky and glowing under red lanterns. Sit at Kabuto (five seats, grilled eel skewers — unforgettable) and order their set menu. Eat with your hands.

    Cash only in most stalls. ¥3,000 – ¥5,000 per person.
Unplanned on purpose

If anything tempts you — a stationery shop, a pocket park, a fruit parfait the size of your head — stop. The itinerary is for guidance, not for keeping.

三
Day Three · Thursday
May 14
A day at sea.
Pace Open from 8am, gone by 10pm. A theme-park marathon, done right.

Tokyo DisneySea.

The most beautifully made theme park in the world, and nothing else in Japan quite prepares you for it. DisneySea is what happens when Disney is handed a blank check and a country that takes craft seriously: a Venetian canal that is actually operable, a Middle-Eastern bazaar built to scale, and a new area — Fantasy Springs — that opened in 2024 with a Frozen fjord, Rapunzel's tower, and Peter Pan's Neverland. You will not finish it. Don't try.

  • 07:30

    Taxi to Maihama — or Keiyo Line from Tokyo Station

    A taxi from the hotel is ~¥8,000 and takes 30 – 40 minutes; the train is ¥230 and takes 40 minutes with a walk at the end. For a day that starts this early, spring for the taxi.

  • 08:30

    Gates open — rope drop the new Fantasy Springs area

    The moment you're inside, walk (don't run) straight to Fantasy Springs. If you've pre-booked the Premier Access pass for Anna & Elsa's Frozen Journey, use it now. Otherwise, aim for Peter Pan's Never Land Adventure first — the newest ride, a beautifully rendered flight through the Neverland skies.

    Book Premier Access via the Tokyo Disney Resort app the morning-of, starting at 07:00. Priority Pass (free) opens at 09:00 for others.
  • 11:00

    The originals — Journey to the Center of the Earth, Tower of Terror, Sinbad's Voyage

    Sinbad is the quiet masterpiece of the park — almost no wait, a full Alan Menken score, and animatronics of a quality you'll see nowhere else. Ride it twice.

  • 13:00

    Lunch — the gyoza dog & the tipo torta

    The famous DisneySea popcorn (curry! black pepper! strawberry!) is the baseline, but for an actual meal: the Gyoza Dog at Nautilus Galley (a bao stuffed with a pork dumpling — only at DisneySea), and the Tipo Torta (a savory-sweet flat bread) at Zambini Brothers. For a proper sit-down meal: Restaurant Sakura — yes, there's a traditional Japanese set menu inside an American Waterfront building. It's excellent.

  • 15:00

    A mid-afternoon rest

    Find a quiet bench on the Mediterranean Harbor with a frozen Calpis. Sit for an hour. This is the difference between enjoying DisneySea and merely surviving it.

  • 17:00

    Soaring: Fantastic Flight & 20,000 Leagues

    A flight simulator that is worth all the comparisons to the Epcot version plus some. And the gentle, undersea 20,000 Leagues — which Robin will love for reasons he won't be able to articulate.

  • 19:30

    Believe! Sea of Dreams — the nighttime show

    Claim a spot along Mediterranean Harbor 30 minutes before. Projections on water screens, fireworks, ships sailing through a coordinated light show. It will, honestly, make one of you cry.

  • 21:30

    Home slowly

    Skip one last ride. Buy a popcorn bucket to carry back. Taxi to Kioicho and put your feet up.

Essential prep

Buy 1-Day Passports on the Tokyo Disney Resort website or app two months ahead — they sell out. ¥10,900 per person at standard-date pricing. Download the app before you go; it handles ride waits, food ordering, and the Premier Access system.

四
Day Four · Friday
May 15
Old Tokyo & a dinner you'll remember.
Pace Morning with your hands, afternoon on your feet, evening at the table.

A kitchen, a village, a Michelin two-star.

The most quietly lovely day of the trip. A morning learning to make proper Japanese home cooking in someone's kitchen; an afternoon in Yanaka, the one piece of Tokyo the 20th century mostly left alone; and an evening at Den — the warmest, most mischievous two-starred table in the country, and exactly the right place to spend your big dinner of Tokyo.

  • 09:30

    Private cooking class — Buddha Bellies or Cooking Sun 3 hours

    A small-group class in a real kitchen with a local teacher. The sushi + miso + tamagoyaki class at Buddha Bellies (Iidabashi) or the Cooking Sun obanzai class in Shibuya are both excellent — relaxed, hands-on, and heavy on the eating-what-you-made part. Book a month ahead.

    For Anushka: ask the teacher about dashi — the stock that quietly underwrites almost everything on your plate in Japan.
  • 13:30

    Train to Nippori — the old edge of town

    15 minutes on the Yamanote Line. A different Tokyo waits at the top of the stairs — two-story houses, low wires, cats on windowsills, a thousand tiny shops.

  • 14:00

    Yanaka Ginza & the sunset stairs

    The "Cat-Watching Stairs" descend into Yanaka Ginza — a 170-meter shotengai (shopping street) that has barely changed in 60 years. The snacks are the point. Buy menchi-katsu from Niku no Suzuki, a taiyaki from Yanaka Shippoya (shaped like a cat — Anushka-approved), an iced matcha at Kayaba Coffee, and just keep walking.

  • 15:30

    Nezu Shrine — azalea tunnels

    A 15-minute walk brings you to Nezu, a 1900-year-old shrine with a corridor of vermilion torii gates nearly as striking as Fushimi Inari's, but without a single tour bus. If you're lucky the azaleas are still clinging on — thousands of them, up the hill behind the main hall.

  • 17:00

    Back to the hotel — rest and change

    Dinner has a relaxed dress code — smart casual — but you'll want to arrive unhurried. The taxi from the hotel is 15 minutes.

  • 18:30

    Dinner at DEN — Michelin ★★

    Chef Zaiyu Hasegawa's kaiseki-in-spirit tasting menu is famous for having an actual working salad "garden" that arrives at the table; for the "Dentucky Fried Chicken" in its little paper bucket; for a rice course that people talk about for years afterward. It is also, and this is the part most critics undersell — the friendliest two-star in Tokyo. Chef Hasegawa's wife Emi will bring out their dog mid-service if she thinks you're in the right mood.

    ¥38,000 per person for the tasting menu. Book 60 days ahead via Pocket Concierge or TableAll. Roughly 2.5 – 3 hours.
  • 22:00

    A slow walk home

    Through Jingumae's dark streets. A convenience-store ice cream on the way — strawberry Pino, trust me — if you have the room.

If Den is full

Ask the hotel concierge about Florilège (French-Japanese, 2★), Sézanne (modern French, 3★), or Ginza Kojyu (traditional kaiseki, 2★). Any of them will do beautifully.

五
Day Five · Saturday
May 16
A day of bright pleasures.
Pace Playful. Ticketed in the evening. Long lunch break in the middle.

Kirby, Mario, Pokémon & the forever museum.

Shamelessly indulgent: a day built around the things that are ours. Pokémon Center in the morning, a pink pastel Kirby lunch at noon, Nintendo's flagship store in the afternoon, and the evening at teamLab Borderless — the digital-art museum that reopened in 2024 inside Azabudai Hills, and that will do to your senses what nothing else in this itinerary will attempt.

  • 10:00

    Pokémon Center Shibuya Parco 6F

    Enter through the automatic doors as Mewtwo is frozen in a glass tank above you. An hour is plenty — say hello, buy the small things (not the giant plush), and move on.

  • 11:15

    Nintendo Tokyo Parco 6F · same building

    Directly across the hall. Life-size Link, a Yoshi in a corner, embroidered cap-badges of every Mario suit. This is the best of the three Nintendo stores in the world. Budget accordingly.

  • 13:00

    Lunch at Kirby Café Tokyo Tokyo Skytree Town

    30 minutes east by Metro. The entire café is pink. The Waddle Dee curry has a Waddle Dee drawn on the rice; the Maxim Tomato pasta comes under a cheese dome that is pulled off at the table. It is — this cannot be overstated — one of the few themed cafés in the world that is also unironically a good lunch.

    Reservations open one month ahead to the day at 18:00 JST via the Kirby Café website. They fill in minutes. Set a reminder.
  • 15:00

    Tokyo Skytree observation deck — if the sky is clear

    You're in the building already. It's the tallest tower in Japan; go up if the day is blue, skip if it's hazy. Buy tickets at the counter.

  • 16:30

    A café hour in Azabudai Hills

    Metro back west. Azabudai Hills is Tokyo's newest mega-complex and quietly one of its most beautiful. Coffee and a slice of something at Bricolage Bread & Co. in the low garden floor. Then saunter — literally, as slowly as you can — around the plantings until your teamLab slot.

  • 18:30

    teamLab Borderless — the reopened museum

    A 90-minute labyrinth of projections, mirrored rooms, and digital flowers that bloom where you stand and follow you between rooms. There is no map. Wear a light color and you'll see why it's worth it. Book the last evening slot of the day — the galleries are noticeably emptier after 18:00.

    ¥5,800 per person. Book on teamlab.art two weeks ahead. The shoe lockers are your friend.
  • 20:30

    A quiet dinner — Uobei Shibuya or a neighborhood soba

    After teamLab, nobody wants a ceremony. Uobei (conveyor-belt sushi on a little monorail — ¥120 a plate, delightful) or the soba counter Sarashina Horii in Azabu-Juban. Either will be open past 22:00.

For packing the checked bag

Nintendo Tokyo ships internationally for the larger items — ask for the overseas shipping counter. You don't have to drag a 1:1 Yoshi plush through Osaka.

Day Six · Sunday
May 17
A slow morning; the bullet train; neon.
Pace Half-day travel, evening on the promenade. Luggage handled ahead.

Tokyo to Osaka by Nozomi.

The Shinkansen is the easy part of travel day. The hard part is remembering to breathe before you board. We'll leave at lunchtime, which gives you the morning to pack unhurriedly, and we'll have your big luggage sent directly to the Osaka hotel so you only carry a small overnight bag onto the train. An elegant system that has saved more marriages than all of Kyoto's shrines combined.

  • The night before

    Yamato luggage forwarding — takkyūbin

    Hand large bags to the Prince Gallery concierge before 14:00 on Day 5 with the Osaka hotel's address (provided at check-in). ¥2,200 per bag. They arrive at your next room before you do.

  • 09:00

    Breakfast — the hotel's Japanese set

    Floor 30 at the Prince Gallery. Grilled salmon, miso, tofu, rice, pickles, tea. A proper Japanese breakfast and a genuine skill the hotel is known for.

  • 11:30

    Taxi to Shinagawa Station

    20 minutes. Shinagawa is the easier Shinkansen platform than Tokyo Station — one escalator, two gates, you're on the train.

  • 12:30

    Boarding the Nozomi to Shin-Osaka

    2 hours 25 minutes. Book the E-side window seats (A or E depending on direction) for the Mount Fuji view at about 40 minutes in — she's shy; you'll catch her through a cloud or not at all. Bento from the station's Ekibenya Matsuri (platform 9 concourse): the Makunouchi set is the right answer for a first time.

    A Green Car upgrade (first class) is ~¥5,000 and worth it for the space and quiet.
  • 15:00

    Arrive Shin-Osaka — one JR stop to your hotel

    Four minutes south on JR to Osaka Station. The Osaka Station Hotel, Autograph Collection is reached from the station concourse without ever stepping outside — the elevators are signed in English and take you straight up. Check in. Breathe.

  • 17:30

    Into Dotonbori — Midosuji Line to Namba

    The Midosuji subway entrance is inside the station, directly below the hotel. Six stops, ten minutes, ¥240. Osaka announces itself in neon — the running Glico man, the mechanical crab, billboards the size of billboards. This is the street you came for.

  • 19:00

    Dinner by the piece — a Dotonbori street crawl

    Not one dinner — five small ones, in any order:

    · Takoyaki at Kukuru (the one with the giant octopus over the door).
    · Okonomiyaki at Mizuno — Bib Gourmand, third generation, the yamaimo-baked version.
    · Kushikatsu (panko-fried skewers of vegetables & pork) at Daruma — original Shinsekai location if you have the energy, or the Dotonbori branch if you don't.
    · A Horai 551 pork bun to go, from the stall inside Namba station.
    · For dessert: Pablo half-baked cheesecake, or Rikuro Ojisan's original ¥965 cheesecake, still warm.

    Do not queue for anything over 30 minutes. Osaka will hand you the same food six doors down.
A quiet possibility

If the evening wants softening: the Tombori River Cruise is 20 minutes, gentle, and sees the neon from the water. ¥1,500.

七
Day Seven · Monday
May 18
Kyoto, the first time.
Pace Long day. Start pre-dawn. Reward: kaiseki.

Fushimi Inari before breakfast; Kyoto after.

The secret to Kyoto is to be awake earlier than the tour buses. We'll catch the 07:00 JR Special Rapid from Osaka Station (downstairs from your room, conveniently) to Kyoto, hop the Nara Line two stops south to Fushimi Inari, walk the torii gates in the pale morning, and be back in central Kyoto for a late breakfast before most visitors have finished theirs. The rest of the day unfolds in Gion's lanes, ends with an arrival-by-taxi at Kikunoi — a three-Michelin-star kaiseki table in a house from 1912, which is the dinner you'll want to tell people about for years.

  • 07:00

    JR Special Rapid to Kyoto — straight from the hotel

    Take the elevator down from the Osaka Station Hotel directly to the JR platforms. Special Rapid to Kyoto is 28 minutes, ¥580, tap your Suica — no ticket counter. Change to the Nara Line at Kyoto; two stops south to Inari Station.

  • 08:00

    Fushimi Inari Taisha — the 10,000 gates 90 minutes up & down

    Walk the torii-lined trail all the way to the Yotsutsuji viewpoint, not just the first bend. It takes 40 minutes up. The crowds turn around at the first fork — you will have most of the mountain to yourselves.

  • 10:30

    Late breakfast — Inari-zushi at Neneya

    Fried tofu pouches stuffed with sweetly seasoned rice — the food the foxes at Fushimi Inari, symbolically, want. Neneya, at the foot of the shrine, is the one. Sit on the wooden bench under the plum tree.

  • 11:30

    Bus to Kiyomizu-dera & the slope up

    Tax the knees a little. The approach streets — Sannenzaka and Ninenzaka — are the most photographed old streets in Kyoto, for legitimate reasons. Stop for a yuzu shaved ice, a kimono-rental salesman's pitch, a Totoro-themed plush shop. It's touristy. It's also the thing you came for.

  • 13:30

    Lunch at Nishiki Market Kyoto's kitchen

    A narrow covered market five blocks long, 400 years in business. Eat one thing at each stall as you move north: a warm dashimaki tamago (rolled omelet) at Miki Keiran, yuba (tender tofu skin) at Yuba Yoshi, a skewer of grilled chicken, matcha warabi mochi at Nagatamachi, black sesame soft serve at Konnamonja.

    A pair of free hours after this — shops, the river, Ponto-chō lane.
  • 16:00

    An unhurried walk through Gion

    Down Hanami-koji and the willows of Shirakawa. If you see a geiko walking quickly between tea houses at dusk, she is headed to work — please don't photograph her. The magic here is not the celebrity-sightings; it's the wooden walls, the darkening sky, and the lantern-lit stone bridge across the Shirakawa stream.

  • 18:00

    Dinner at Kikunoi Honten — Michelin ★★★

    The old house in eastern Kyoto that defines modern kaiseki. Chef Yoshihiro Murata has trained most of the kaiseki chefs of your generation — and his own son runs the kitchen now, in service of a tasting menu that moves with the season. For May: a clear soup with young bamboo, fava beans, seasonal river fish, the first peaches. Eight or nine courses, each a small painting.

    ¥44,000 per person (¥48,000 on weekends). Book via the hotel concierge at least 60 days ahead, or by email to the restaurant directly. Roughly 2.5 hours. Smart dress. Closed irregular Tuesdays — confirm your date.
  • 21:30

    Back to Osaka

    Taxi to Kyoto Station, JR Special Rapid back to Osaka Station, elevator up. 45 minutes door to door. Ride home in the happy daze of a long meal.

八
Day Eight · Tuesday
May 19
Kyoto, at the edges.
Pace Train-heavy but on purpose. A short afternoon off.

Bamboo, a golden pavilion, tea.

Today is Kyoto's greatest hits in a loop that actually makes sense geographically. We start at Arashiyama, to the west, with the bamboo grove at the hour it's meant to be seen; we cross the city to Kinkaku-ji, the Golden Pavilion, which is exactly as golden as the postcards; and we finish with a late-afternoon matcha ceremony in a 200-year-old tea room on the Philosopher's Path. Dinner is gentle — soba, home.

  • 07:30

    JR to Saga-Arashiyama

    Direct from Osaka Station — the JR Sagano Line, one train, ~55 minutes. The bamboo grove is a 10-minute walk from the station.

  • 09:00

    The Arashiyama Bamboo Grove

    Not very long. It's a corridor, not a forest. But the light filtering through in the morning is the point. Walk it quietly; the grove is also acoustically remarkable — the Japanese government has listed it as one of the country's 100 Soundscapes.

  • 09:45

    Tenryū-ji & its garden

    At the end of the bamboo path. A 14th-century Zen temple with a pond garden that is considered one of the oldest surviving in Japan — Sogen Pond, framed by the mountains, a composition unchanged for 700 years. Pay the extra ¥300 for garden + temple access; sit on the engawa for ten minutes with your shoes off.

  • 11:30

    A snack on Togetsukyō Bridge

    A roasted sweet potato in a paper wrapper; a cup of matcha soft serve from the shop on the river side. Sit by the water a minute.

  • 12:30

    Lunch — Shigetsu inside Tenryū-ji

    Shōjin ryōri — Buddhist temple vegetarian cuisine. Seven small dishes in lacquer boxes, served in a room facing the garden. ¥4,500. Unusual and memorable; Anushka especially will want to know how the tofu is made.

    Reserve ahead (tenryuji.com). Otherwise: Ramen Arashiyama, a counter by the station.
  • 14:00

    Taxi to Kinkaku-ji, the Golden Pavilion

    25 minutes across the city. There's no route through this on public transport that isn't irritating — take the cab. The temple itself is a 25-minute visit; you walk a loop around the pond, photograph the pavilion, and leave. That is the correct way to do Kinkaku-ji.

  • 15:30

    Matcha tea ceremony on the Philosopher's Path

    Taxi to Camellia Flower Tea Ceremony (Ginkaku-ji location) or En Tea House — both offer private 45-minute ceremonies in English for two, in real tatami rooms, with a proper host. ¥5,500 per person. You will learn to whisk matcha properly. Anushka will not want to leave.

  • 17:00

    The Philosopher's Path — a stroll to close the day

    A two-kilometer canal-side footpath, lined with cherry trees in April and maples in autumn; in May it's green and empty. Walk it as slowly as you like. It ends, eventually, at the silver pavilion Ginkaku-ji — more subtle than its gold cousin, and more beloved by the Japanese for being so.

  • 19:00

    Back to Osaka; quiet dinner near the hotel

    You will be tired. Honke Owariya has a branch near Umeda — Kyoto soba, since you're in the mood. Or ask the concierge for a neighborhood kappo counter within ten minutes on foot.

Grace note

If Arashiyama feels like enough for the day, skip Kinkaku-ji and take the Sagano Scenic Romantic Train instead — a 25-minute open-air train ride along the Hozugawa river gorge. ¥620. Exactly the right way to end a morning in the bamboo.

Nara in the morning; an Osaka afternoon of your own.

An unapologetically cute morning — Nara's sacred deer are semi-wild, will bow for a shika-senbei cracker, and outnumber the tourists before 11:00. After the deer, the Great Buddha: a fifteen-meter-tall bronze statue in the world's largest wooden building. After that, Osaka is yours. Shop, wander, have a long coffee, an unhurried afternoon in. This is the day I've left you to discover your own Japan.

  • 08:30

    45 minutes from Osaka Station. A quiet train with low farmland through the window. Your IC card pays.

    They will come to you. Buy shika-senbei crackers from one of the hundred old women selling them (¥200 a bundle). Hold one up; bow; the deer bows back. Hand it over before the whole herd descends. This is Anushka's morning.

Part Three

Moving through
Japan.

Three things make the country easy: the Suica card on your phone, a little cash in small bills, and the Shinkansen reservation app. Set the first two up before you arrive, and you will spend your trip doing things rather than figuring out how to do them.

Payments — card, cash, and the rule of two pockets

Japan is now largely card-accepting at hotels, department stores, restaurants of any stature, and most chain retailers. Where it isn't accepting: small yakitori stalls, some temple fees, family restaurants, the fruit man at Nishiki. Assume roughly one in five places wants cash. A target of ¥20,000 per person in small bills at any given time is right; top up every few days.

Setting up before you fly

  • Add a Suica card to Apple Wallet now (iPhone Wallet → "+" → Transit Card → Suica). Takes 60 seconds, costs nothing. Load it with a credit card as you go. Taps onto all trains, buses, vending machines, and most convenience stores. The single most useful thing you will do before this trip.
  • Pre-authorize your credit cards for Japan with your bank. Foreign transactions from a sudden location trigger fraud locks more often than people realize.
  • Carry one Visa and one Mastercard. Amex is accepted at the Prince Gallery, the Osaka Station Hotel, Kikunoi and Den — and at fewer places in between than you'd assume.
  • No tipping. Not at restaurants, not in taxis, not at the hotel. Attempting to tip creates small awkwardness. The price is the price.

Getting yen on the ground

  • 7-Eleven ATMs (the red one inside every 7-Eleven) accept all foreign cards, run in English, and are everywhere. Japan Post ATMs also. Narita has them airside.
  • Don't exchange money at the airport counter — the rate is poor. ATMs are 1 – 2% better.
  • A ¥10,000 note from an ATM feels very large; break it at a convenience store on a drink or a pack of gum before you need it in a stall.
The Suica trick

Your Suica on Apple Wallet can be tapped to pay at almost every convenience store, vending machine, and many small restaurants — without even unlocking your phone. Keep ¥5,000 – ¥10,000 loaded on it at all times; top up with Apple Pay. This will handle ~30% of your purchases without you ever reaching for a card or cash.

Public transit — three cities, one card

Your Suica card works in every major city in Japan including all three on your itinerary — Tokyo Metro, Toei lines, JR lines, Osaka Metro, Kyoto city buses and subway, even on the trains to the airports. Tap on, tap off. No ticket machines, ever, unless you want to.

In Tokyo

  • Google Maps is stunningly accurate — it gives platform numbers, exit letters, and walking directions from within the station. Trust it.
  • The nearest station to the Prince Gallery is Nagatachō (Yurakuchō, Hanzōmon, Namboku lines — a 3-minute walk) or Akasaka-mitsuke (Marunouchi, Ginza — 6 minutes).
  • Rush hour is 07:30 – 09:30 and 17:30 – 19:30 on weekdays. Avoid the Yamanote Line at these times if you can — it is every bit as dense as you've seen in videos.
  • Taxis are plentiful, professional, and not cheap. A cross-city ride is ¥3,000 – ¥5,000. Use the GO app (free; downloads instantly) to summon one — it pays directly from your credit card, no language needed.

In Osaka

  • The Midosuji Line (red) is Osaka's main artery. The entrance is directly below your hotel. It connects Shin-Osaka (Shinkansen), Umeda (your station and the department stores), Honmachi, Shinsaibashi (shopping), and Namba (Dotonbori) in a single 20-minute stretch.
  • Taxis are cheaper than Tokyo's. A Dotonbori-to-hotel ride is ¥1,200 – ¥1,800.
  • Osaka's JR Loop Line also runs everywhere — useful for the day trips to Nara, Kyoto, and USJ, all of which leave directly from Osaka Station under your feet.

In Kyoto

  • Kyoto's subway only has two lines; the city is mainly navigated by buses. They accept your Suica. Bus 206 is the big-hitter — it loops past most temples.
  • Taxis are the right answer for anything that isn't on the first try. Kyoto buses get crowded, slow, and unhelpful in mid-afternoon. A cross-city cab is ¥1,500 – ¥2,500. The GO app works here, too.
  • For the walking neighborhoods — Gion, Philosopher's Path, Arashiyama, Higashiyama — you will barely need transit at all. Walk between the temples; the distances are shorter than they look.
A warning worth printing

On escalators in Tokyo, stand on the left. In Osaka, stand on the right. It's the opposite of almost every other country in the world, and Osaka is the opposite of Tokyo. Nobody will say anything; everyone will notice.

Between cities — Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka

The Shinkansen is on the Tokaido Line, which runs all three of your cities in a straight shot. Book reserved seats on the SmartEX app (free; foreign credit cards welcome), which lets you scan directly at the ticket gates with your phone — no paper, no counter. Do this a day or two ahead.

Should we buy the Japan Rail Pass?

For this itinerary, no. The pass was repriced in late 2023 (now ¥50,000 for 7 days) and no longer pays for itself on a two-city trip. Buy individual Shinkansen tickets on SmartEX; you'll spend ~¥30,000 total on long-distance trains and save the difference for sushi.

Shinkansen etiquette, briefly

  • Reserved seats only. (Non-reserved are cheaper, standing often required on popular trains.)
  • Phone on silent; no calls. Quiet carriage.
  • Eating is expected and encouraged — this is what ekiben are for. Don't be shy with the rice box.
  • Luggage: oversized bags (over 160cm summed) must be reserved with the ticket. Your normal suitcase is fine without it. There are racks at the end of each carriage.

Apps before you fly

  • Google Maps — train navigation, platforms, exits.
  • SmartEX — Shinkansen reservations, IC-pass scanning.
  • GO — Japan's Uber equivalent; default taxi app.
  • Tokyo Disney Resort — Premier / Priority passes, ride times.
  • Universal Studios Japan — timed-entry tickets for Super Nintendo World.
  • Pocket Concierge — reservations for Den, kaiseki, high-end sushi.
  • Google Translate — camera mode for menus; download the Japanese pack offline.
  • Papago — a second translator; weirdly better at food menus than Google.

Phrases worth learning

  • Sumimasen — "excuse me / sorry / thank you all at once." The most useful word in Japanese; say it 40 times a day.
  • Arigatō gozaimasu — thank you, properly.
  • Onegai shimasu — "please, if you would." Softens every request.
  • Oishii! — "delicious." Say it unprompted and the chef will beam.
  • Okaikei onegaishimasu — "check, please."
  • Eigo no menyū wa arimasu ka? — "is there an English menu?"
  • Daijōbu desu — "I'm fine / no thank you / all good." A universal polite deflection.

One small packing note

May in Tokyo and Kansai is warm but not hot — averages of 23°C by day, 16°C at night, with a chance of a warm rain. Pack light layers; a thin rain shell that folds into itself; comfortable walking shoes that slip off easily (you will take them off roughly ten times, at shrines, temples, some restaurants, and a tea room). An unobtrusive small daypack; a travel umbrella; a reusable water bottle. Leave the iron. Every hotel has one.

Two Japanese rules worth knowing

  • Don't eat while walking. A stall will often have a small stand beside it to eat at; use it. (Exception: ice cream, and anything bought at Yanaka Ginza.)
  • Don't blow your nose in public. Excuse yourself to a bathroom. It is a bigger deal than you'd expect; it's also fine to sniffle in front of anyone.
